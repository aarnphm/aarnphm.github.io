_ex opere operato_, "from the work worked." sacraments produce their effect through the ritual itself, independent of the participant's worthiness. baptism regenerates you whether or not you believe it does. communion sanctifies you whether or not you feel sanctified. the ceremony acts on you. the protestant objection was always the same: no, grace requires faith. the ritual doesn't work mechanically. you have to actually mean it.

philip pullman wrote a version of this problem as fiction. in _the good man jesus and the scoundrel christ_, jesus and christ are twin brothers [@pullman2010goodman]. jesus is the genuine ethical teacher, radical, refuses to build institutions, says things that make the powerful uncomfortable. christ is the one who follows him around recording everything, editing the story, adding miracles where there were coincidences, shaping the narrative so it can survive as an institution. a stranger visits christ and tells him the institution will betray the original message, but the institution is necessary for the message to survive. you get preservation at the cost of hollowing.

i have been thinking about this trade bc i took the iron ring obligation last year and something about it has been sitting wrong with me since.

## haultain's tribal spirit

on january 25, 1922, a mining engineer named herbert haultain stood before the engineering institute of canada and delivered a speech called "the romance of engineering." the word he kept returning to was "tribal spirit." he wanted engineers to feel like a tribe. the quebec bridge had collapsed fifteen years earlier, killing 75 workers (33 of them mohawk ironworkers from kahnawake, a fact the ceremony would later omit), and haultain's stated motivation was preventing another quebec bridge.[^bridge] but the language he chose tells you what he was actually after. he didn't say "ethical rigor" or "professional standards." he said tribal spirit.

[^bridge]: the bridge collapsed twice, 1907 and 1916. the first collapse killed 75. the cause was straightforward: the chief engineer, theodore cooper, ignored evidence that compression chords were buckling under the bridge's own weight. workers raised concerns. the concerns were dismissed. 75 people fell into the st. lawrence. the second collapse during reconstruction killed 13 more. you'd think 88 deaths across two tries would produce more than a ceremony.

haultain contacted rudyard kipling in october 1923, asking him to write the ritual. kipling, the man who wrote "the white man's burden," who was described by the _new yorker_ as "variously labelled a colonialist, a jingoist, a racist, an anti-semite, a misogynist." the first ceremony happened on april 25, 1925, at the university club in montreal. six engineers attended.

there is a myth that the original iron rings were forged from the wreckage of the quebec bridge. they weren't. they were manufactured by world war i veterans at christie street military hospital in toronto.[^myth] the myth matters more than the material. 500,000 engineers believe they wear failure on their hands. they wear stainless steel manufactured in a hospital. the ceremony's founding story is itself a fabrication in service of tribal identity, which is exactly what pullman's christ does to jesus's teachings: edits the record, adds symbolic weight where there was only accident.

[^myth]: the rings were originally iron (which tarnished and left marks) but are now stainless steel. kipling designed the rough facets, which he said symbolized the unrefined character of young engineers. the ring goes on the little finger of the working hand, where it drags across paper when you sign drawings. over decades of use, the facets wear smooth. kipling built a physical metaphor for how competence shades into complacency, the sharp edges of new obligation becoming the frictionless surface of long habit. or maybe it's just material science. either way, the ring stops reminding you.

## the grift

the thing that bothers me about the ring is the speed of the transition. you stand in a room and recite words written by a victorian imperialist about honour and cold iron, and it moves you, or at least it moved me, and then within weeks the ring becomes a conversation piece at job fairs. i've watched people flash it at recruiters the way you'd flash a rolex. the ceremony produces genuine emotional intensity (i'm not denying this, i felt it) and then the social machinery of engineering culture converts that intensity into a credential.

the "good times in school" conversation is revealing. when engineers talk about their rings, they talk about the ceremony as a shared experience, the friends who were there, the feeling of completion. they almost never talk about the obligation itself, the specific ethical content, the commitment to refuse "bad workmanship or faulty material." the ring becomes a nostalgia anchor for the social experience of engineering school rather than an ethical anchor for the practice of engineering.

## the ceremony

the obligation of the calling of an engineer is, by its own description, "not an oath but a solemn expression of intention." i want you to sit with that phrase for a second. the corporation of the seven wardens, the body that administers the ceremony across 28 camps in canada, explicitly distinguishes the obligation from an oath. an oath binds you to a fact: i swear this is true. an expression of intention describes a feeling: i intend to do this.

the full text:

> I, ____________, in the presence of these my betters and my equals in my Calling, bind myself upon my Honour and Cold Iron, that, to the best of my knowledge and power, I will not henceforward suffer or pass, or be privy to the passing of, Bad Workmanship or Faulty Material in aught that concerns my works before mankind as an Engineer, or in my dealings with my conscience. My time I will not refuse; my Thought I will not grudge; my Care I will not deny towards the honour, use, stability and perfection of any works to which I may be called to set my hand.

kipling's prose has weight. the archaisms ("henceforward," "aught," "compass or wrest") create the feeling of ancient tradition in something that was written in 1923. the ceremony is semi-secret, private, attended only by engineers and those being called.[^secrecy] it functions as what [[thoughts/performativity|butler]] would recognize as a constitutive performance: you don't take the obligation because you're an engineer. you become an engineer (in the tribal sense) by taking it. the speech act creates the identity it appears to describe.[^turner]

[^secrecy]: the semi-secrecy does specific work. it creates in-group cohesion ([[thoughts/performativity#goffman: dramaturgical sociology|goffman's]] front-stage/back-stage) and makes the ring function as a tribal marker rather than a public ethical standard. the hippocratic oath is public. the iron ring obligation is semi-private. the difference matters for accountability. you can hold someone to a public oath. a private expression of intention is between you and your conscience, which is to say, it's between you and nothing verifiable.

[^turner]: victor turner's concept of [[thoughts/performativity#civilisation requires performance|liminality]] maps cleanly onto the ceremony [@turner1969ritual]. the participants are "betwixt and between," stripped of their student identity, not yet incorporated as engineers. the communitas generated in this liminal state is real. but communitas is a feeling, and feelings are what emotivism trades in.

## on moral emotivism

a.j. ayer, in _language, truth and logic_, argued that moral judgments express attitudes rather than state facts [@ayer1936language]. "stealing is wrong" doesn't describe a property of stealing. it expresses disapproval. it's equivalent to saying "stealing, boo!" c.l. stevenson refined this: ethical language has both emotive meaning (expressing the speaker's attitude) and imperative meaning (aimed at changing the listener's feelings) [@stevenson1944ethics]. moral statements are tools for influencing attitudes, not reports about the world.

the obligation is emotivist by its own terms. "not an oath but a solemn expression of intention." this is stevenson's framework stated plainly. the obligation expresses the speaker's attitude toward bad workmanship AND aims to influence their future behavior through emotional commitment. but an expression of intention with no enforcement mechanism is a feeling.[^ayer-bridge]

[^ayer-bridge]: ayer's verification principle was designed to eliminate theology. but he realized it also eliminated moral cognitivism. as i noted in my [[hinterland/research/analytic-atheism-research|research on analytic atheism]]: "ayer had to handle ethics with emotivism. this puts theism in the same boat as morality. if eliminating theism is a win, you've also eliminated moral cognitivism." the iron ring ceremony sits at the intersection: quasi-religious in structure (semi-secret, administered by wardens, centered on an obligation), moral in content. ayer's framework makes both the religious form and the ethical content non-cognitive. the ceremony is doubly emotivist.

alasdair macintyre's diagnosis in _after virtue_ goes further [@macintyre1981aftervirtue]. he argues that contemporary moral culture is "a theater of illusions in which objective moral rhetoric masks arbitrary choices." emotivism, for macintyre, "entails the obliteration of any genuine distinction between manipulative and non-manipulative social relations." the ceremony manipulates you into feeling obligated, and this feeling IS the obligation. there is no fact underneath the feeling.

macintyre identifies three "characters" of emotivist culture: the manager, the therapist, the rich aesthete. all three claim value-neutral expertise while operating within an emotivist moral order. the engineer belongs in this company. the manager transforms investment into profit while treating the end as given, outside his scope. the engineer transforms requirements into structures while treating the question of WHETHER to build as someone else's problem (what the frontiers paper calls "social captivity"). the obligation SOUNDS like a factual commitment ("i will not suffer bad workmanship") but functions as attitude expression. the engineer-character claims technical objectivity while embedded in a moral framework that, by its own admission, traffics in expressions of intention rather than binding commitments.

i keep coming back to the dilution problem. even if emotivism could work as a behavioral nudge (thaler and sunstein have shown that physical symbols can shift behavior, and the ring on the writing hand is a constant tactile reminder), the social reality of engineering culture strips the ring of its emotivist function. when nobody around you takes the [[thoughts/ethics|ethics]] seriously, when the ring is a credential rather than a commitment, the emotive meaning evaporates. the framework isn't wrong. it's diluted. the social conditions required for emotivism to function (collective commitment to the attitude being expressed) have decayed into credentialism.

the frontiers paper on the ceremony found NO empirical evidence that the ritual produces measurable ethical behavior change [@crossman2023stubborn]. zero. the authors concluded that the ceremony "has served to reproduce and map boundaries around engineering ethics and responsibility in canada" rather than producing ethical transformation. it maps the boundary. it doesn't cross it.

## institutional delusion

the profession-wide belief that the ring produces ethical engineers is maintained at scale despite zero evidence. this is r.m. hare's "blik" applied to engineering: an unfalsifiable attitude that structures experience.[^hare] "i will not suffer bad workmanship" can't be falsified. what would count as evidence that you've violated it? the ring structures your self-perception as an ethical engineer without any test condition. flew's invisible gardener is wearing an iron ring.

[^hare]: from the theology and falsification symposium (1950). hare argued that religious claims aren't assertions but unfalsifiable attitudes that structure experience. everyone has a blik. the lunatic who thinks dons want to kill him has a blik too. the question isn't WHETHER to have one but WHICH one. the iron ring obligation is a blik about engineering ethics. and flew's challenge applies: the obligation "dies by a thousand qualifications." each apparent counterexample gets absorbed. the engineer who passed bad workmanship still has the ring. the ring doesn't come off.

500,000 people have taken the obligation. how many bridge collapses, environmental disasters, and systemic failures have happened since 1925? the ring didn't prevent them. but the profession maintains the belief system bc abandoning it would require admitting the ceremony is social theater.

in april 2025, on the ceremony's 100th anniversary, the corporation of the seven wardens launched a modernized version. they removed religious imagery and gendered language. they commissioned two new poems (one english, one french) to replace kipling's text. chris hadfield delivered a modern interpretation of the obligation. but they preserved the obligation text itself.

this is the tell. the wardens understand, implicitly, that the ethical content isn't in the specific words. the kipling poetry was decorative. the surrounding text was replaceable. what matters is the ritual structure, the liminal experience, the ring placement, the communitas. but if the words don't matter, what does? the answer is: the social cohesion, the tribal identity, the credential. the ceremony's actual function has drifted from "ethical commitment" to "professional incorporation" (a dynamic that runs through [[posts/occupational licensure|the history of PEO]] more broadly), and the 2025 modernization codifies this drift by treating the surrounding text as scenery.

durkheim explains why the delusion persists [@durkheim1912elementary]. the ceremony generates genuine [[thoughts/performativity#political dimensions|collective effervescence]], emotional intensity that FEELS like moral transformation. i felt it. but feeling ≠ fact. this is emotivism's own internal critique made manifest: if moral statements are expressions of feeling, then the ceremony is doing exactly what it was designed to do (producing feeling). the delusion is expecting it to do anything more.

pullman's twin brothers come back to me here. haultain was jesus, the genuine ethical teacher who saw 75 people fall into the st. lawrence and wanted to prevent it from happening again. the ceremony is christ, the institution-builder who took that impulse and turned it into something that could survive a hundred years, 28 camps, 500,000 participants, a royal canadian mint commemorative coin. the institution preserved the concern. it also hollowed it out. christ always does this to jesus. the stranger told him it would happen. it happened anyway.

i still wear the ring. i notice it when i type, when i pick things up, when i'm in the shower. haultain wanted tribal spirit and he got it. the ring makes me feel like an engineer. whether it makes me a better one is a question no ceremony can answer for me.
