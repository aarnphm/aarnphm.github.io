In April 2026, a year after the centennial year of the obligation of the calling of an engineer, I recited words written by Rudyard Kipling in 1923.

The Corporation of the Seven Wardens, the body that administers the ceremony across 28 camps in Canada, had spent the previous twelve months preparing the change. Their changes include removing the religious imagery and the gendered language included in within previous ceremonial procedure. They also commissioned two new poems, one English and one French, to replace Kipling's text in the surrounding ritual. Bringing in Chris Hadfield, the astronaut, to deliver a modern interpretation of the obligation was a part of it. Though, they did preserve the central text—written by the same Kipling who wrote "The White Man's Burden"—verbatim.

There are approximately 500,000 engineers taken this obligation since 1925. I am now, one of them. The ring has been on my pinky finger for the last four months, alongside the full set of jewelries I wore daily.

---

The phrase _ex opere operato_ is from medieval sacramental theology and I have been chewing on it since the ceremony, on the GO train home and at the kitchen counter at 2am and at the bar on Locke when the second drink had finished doing its work. From the work worked. A sacrament does its work through the performance, regardless of whether the priest is in a state of grace or the recipient believes the right things. The infant baptized cannot consent and the sacrament works on him anyway. The Reformers fought this position for two centuries. Grace required faith, they said, and a ritual a man did not mean was no ritual at all.

The iron ring obligation tries for both at once. The engineer takes it, feels what he feels in the room, and the wardens have arranged things so no court will ever be in a position to require him to produce that feeling later.

There's a Pullman novel I read last fall, on a library copy I owe the McMaster stacks and have not gotten around to returning. _The Good Man Jesus and the Scoundrel Christ_ [@pullman2010goodman]. In it Jesus has a twin brother and the brother's name is Christ. Christ follows Jesus around with a wax tablet, writing down what Jesus says, except occasionally Christ writes down what he thinks Jesus should have said instead, which is the part of the novel that did me in. There's a chapter somewhere in the back half where a stranger comes to Christ at night. The stranger tells him plainly that the institution Christ is building on his brother's name is going to betray the message, and that if the institution doesn't betray it, the message will die with Jesus. Christ knows this. He keeps writing the slightly edited Jesus down anyway. I read the chapter at the kitchen counter (the same counter where I'm writing this now, which I noticed only later) and then I went to bed because there wasn't anything else I wanted to do that night.

The wardens preserved the obligation verbatim and revised everything around it. I have been thinking about that trade since.

<!-- haultain's tribal spirit -->

I went looking after the ceremony. Slow Saturday, laptop sitting on top of a textbook I hadn't opened in weeks. There's a PDF of the original 1922 talk on the Engineering Institute of Canada's website, scanned out of typescript with the page numbers still visible in the corners. Herbert Haultain gave it on January 25 1922 under the title "The Romance of Engineering." He was a mining engineer who taught at the University of Toronto, fifty-three years old at the time. I read the talk twice because I wasn't sure the first time whether the phrase he keeps returning to, _tribal spirit_, was actually his or whether I'd put it in his mouth. It was his.

The bridge that pulls the talk into focus is the Quebec Bridge, which gave way in August 1907 under its own weight before construction had finished, with workers still on it.[^bridge] Seventy-five men into the water. I kept getting the number wrong on the first few read-throughs and had to check it. Thirty-three of the seventy-five were Mohawk ironworkers out of Kahnawake, which I had to look up separately, and which the ceremony does not name. Haultain would have been somewhere around forty in 1907, by then enough of a career engineer to be paying attention. The line he uses about why he wanted the ritual is prevention of a second Quebec Bridge. That is what the page says. Reading the talk the second time, sitting on my apartment floor with the PDF on the laptop next to me, I noticed it wants something else too. I am still not sure exactly how to phrase the something. The closest I can get on the page is engineers who belong to each other the way men in a tribe belong. Born or sworn into a unit, with a line drawn around the unit that has the force of expulsion behind it. The competing option for what the engineering profession could look like in the twenties was the examination route, the way the bar and the medical college were already running. Haultain's talk goes out of its way not to endorse the examination route. The carefulness of the not-endorsement is what stayed with me.

[^bridge]: The Quebec Bridge collapsed twice. In 1907 the cantilever arms gave way during construction because the chief engineer, Theodore Cooper, had been told by his own inspectors that the lower compression chords were buckling and had decided not to halt the work. 75 men were on the bridge when it went into the river. In 1916, during reconstruction, the central span fell during a hoisting operation and killed another 13. 88 dead across two attempts produced one ceremony, which is some kind of ratio.

Haultain wrote to Kipling in October 1923 from somewhere in Toronto, asking for help. The letter is in the McGill archives, which I have not been to. What I have to go on is what other people have written about the letter. They say Haultain was asking Kipling for help giving the obligation a literary form Canadian engineers would take seriously. Kipling was the obvious pick. He was the last famous living British poet anyone could think of by 1923, and "The White Man's Burden" was already a quarter-century behind him. McGrath's phrasing for what the reputation was, writing about it in the _New Yorker_ a couple of years ago, runs: variously labelled a colonialist, a jingoist, a racist, an anti-semite, a misogynist. Kipling was alive and willing to take the job. Canadian engineering valued the willingness over whatever it minded about the labels. The first ceremony went off at the University Club in Montreal on April 25, 1925. Six engineers showed up. I come back to that number every time I am tempted to take the centennial seriously, with its commemorative coin from the Royal Mint and its press releases about modernization.

There is the story that the rings were forged from salvaged Quebec Bridge steel. I heard a version of it at a co-op interview in summer 2023, the panel of senior engineers around a glass table on the twelfth floor of a building near King and Bay, the one who told me had no reason to have checked and I had no reason to push back. The rings are not from the wreckage. The early ones were made by First World War veterans at the Christie Street Military Hospital in Toronto, and since the seventies they have been stainless because the original iron stained pinkies dark and people complained.[^myth] What sticks with me is the attachment to the wrong metal. Half a million pinkies in the country with what people think is 1907 wreckage on them, and it is hospital stainless, and nobody I have asked has cared when I have brought this up. The wrong-metal story is the kind of edit Christ keeps making to Jesus in the Pullman novel, a small weight inserted where the original had only an accident.

[^myth]: Kipling designed the rough facets and said in a letter that they were meant to symbolize the unrefined character of the young engineer. The ring goes on the little finger of the working hand, where it scrapes the paper when the engineer signs drawings, and over a working life the facets wear smooth. Whether the metaphor is Kipling's, or whether it is just what happens to small steel objects rubbed against paper for forty years, the ring stops reminding you that it is there.

The ring has been on my pinky for four months and the degree comes in two. Neither ceremony asks whether I meant any of it. After both, _student_ stops being something I can usefully say about myself.

<!-- the grift -->

A normal Tuesday in last year's fall term started at Union on the 6:47am GO, two hours of half-sleep against the window until somewhere past Oakville where the Escarpment shows up. McMaster I passed through more than lived at. Two morning classes I hadn't read for, then the day in the atrium of ITB triaging GitHub issues for Bento[^bento], then the 8pm back to Toronto. On the weekends in Hamilton there was a bar on Locke Street where I'd order pasta and ask the chef what he was testing that week, and an overpriced vegan grocery up the block where the yogurt cost two-point-something times what the same brand cost at the Fortinos two blocks down.

[^bento]: An open-source ML inference platform. I was a maintainer through undergrad: distributed systems, performance, the occasional 3am debugging for users I would never meet.

The ring was on my hand through all of it and I forgot about it most of the time. When I remembered it was because a strap had snagged on it, or because it had clicked against the rim of a glass and I had wondered for half a second what the click was, and then I would remember.

The speed of the conversion is what bothers me. You stand in a room reciting Victorian-imperialist lines about honour and cold iron, and it moves you, or it moved me, and within two months you are tapping the ring against a desk in a recruiter call the way you would tap a Rolex. I did that in November on a call with a startup in Palo Alto. The engineer in the critical paragraph here is me. The feeling in the room was real and by dessert at the dinner afterward the same feeling was already converting into something I could deploy.

At the dinner after my own ceremony, no one said the words "bad workmanship" once. We talked about who got into which company. When two engineers talk about their rings in any room that is not a wardens' room, they end up talking about the dinner, the picture at the door, the friends who were there.

<!-- the ceremony -->

In the Corporation of the Seven Wardens' own description, the obligation of the calling of an engineer is "not an oath but a solemn expression of intention." The Corporation administers the ceremony across 28 camps and has been explicit about the distinction. An oath binds its taker to a fact, with consequences if the fact later turns out false. An expression of intention is a state of mind in a room, and a state of mind in a room is not the kind of object a court can later make the engineer produce.

The text:

> I, ****Aaron Pham****, in the presence of these my betters and my equals in my Calling, bind myself upon my Honour and Cold Iron, that, to the best of my knowledge and power, I will not henceforward suffer or pass, or be privy to the passing of, Bad Workmanship or Faulty Material in aught that concerns my works before mankind as an Engineer, or in my dealings with my conscience. My time I will not refuse; my Thought I will not grudge; my Care I will not deny towards the honour, use, stability and perfection of any works to which I may be called to set my hand.

Kipling's archaisms (_henceforward_, _aught_, _compass or wrest_) give the document a 17th-century weight it does not actually have, since the thing was composed by a Victorian in 1923. The ceremony is semi-secret. Only engineers and the engineers about to become engineers are allowed in the room. [[thoughts/performativity|Butler]]'s constitutive performance fits with one catch: the taking of the obligation creates the membership it claims to describe, and the resulting constitution cannot be undone by a later ceremony in the way Butler's framework otherwise allows.[^secrecy][^turner]

[^secrecy]: The semi-secrecy is doing specific work. The Hippocratic Oath is public, and you can take a doctor to a court that will quote it back at him. The iron ring obligation is private and is between the engineer and his own conscience, which is between him and nothing verifiable. The secrecy turns the ring from a public ethical standard into a tribal marker. [[thoughts/performativity#goffman: dramaturgical sociology|Goffman]] would have called this a backstage rite.

[^turner]: Victor Turner gave the name [[thoughts/performativity#civilisation requires performance|liminality]] to exactly the state the ceremony places its participants in [@turner1969ritual]. They are between the student they have been and the engineer they have not yet been called. The _communitas_ that Turner identified in such states is real, and it was real in the room where I took mine. But _communitas_ is a feeling, and feelings are the thing emotivism is most interested in.

---

I read A.J. Ayer's _Language, Truth and Logic_ [@ayer1936language] in third year, the ethics elective, picked because it fit my timetable around a database systems course I cared about more. Ayer wrote the book at twenty-six. The book was a hit on theology, with most of philosophy of religion taken out in a couple of chapters, and on its own terms it took out moral philosophy as a side effect too. Ayer admitted as much later. The position runs like this. Moral statements have stopped being statements. _Stealing is wrong_, in Ayer's reading, has about the factual content of _boo to stealing_. Stevenson came along a few years later and tightened it [@stevenson1944ethics]. He noticed the moral statement was doing two things at the same time: expressing the speaker's disapproval and trying to pull the listener into disapproving alongside him. That feels closer to how moral talk actually goes in a room. Ayer and Stevenson agreed that whatever the moral statement was doing, it had stopped reporting anything about the world.

On its own constitutional terms, the obligation is emotivist. "Not an oath but a solemn expression of intention" is roughly the Stevenson position rendered as policy. The engineer expresses an attitude toward bad workmanship, and the expression is supposed to influence his own future conduct. An expression of intention with no enforcement attached, though, is just a feeling, and feelings tend not to make it through the year.[^ayer-bridge]

[^ayer-bridge]: Ayer built the verification principle to put theology out of business and was honest enough to notice that he had also put moral cognitivism out of business at the same time. As I wrote in my [[hinterland/research/analytic-atheism-research|notes on analytic atheism]]: theism and morality go in the same boat, and if eliminating theism is a win then you have also eliminated moral cognitivism. The iron ring sits at the intersection of the two. It has a quasi-religious form (semi-secret, administered by wardens, structured around an obligation) and a moral content. Ayer empties both halves at once, which makes the ceremony doubly emotivist and the engineer who took it the inheritor of two things that have been denied any standing for the better part of a century.

_After Virtue_ I got to late. Third-year ethics elective, picked because it fit the timetable around a database systems course I cared more about. MacIntyre wrote it in 1981 [@macintyre1981aftervirtue], and his move there is taking the emotivists at their word and working out what culture follows. If moral language really is only expressions of preference, what gets built on top of that. The thing that gets built, in MacIntyre's reading, is a culture where persuasion has collapsed into manipulation. Persuasion needs a moral fact in the room for both parties to point at; in MacIntyre's culture there are no moral facts by construction, so all you've got left is one party trying to move the other and calling it whatever sounds right. He picks out three character types as the exemplars of this culture (the manager, the therapist, the wealthy aesthete) and the engineer slots in alongside them cleanly enough that I think MacIntyre would have included the engineer too, if he had been working through Canadian examples.

What the engineering job looks like, from where I have been sitting on it, is this. Someone hands you a requirements document. You hand back something built that satisfies the document. Whether the something should have been built in the first place is the client's problem and stops being yours the moment you accept the requirements. A manager has the same setup with investment money. The board hands him a profit target, he works to it, and what the profit is supposed to go toward stays at the board. There is a paper in _Frontiers in Sociology_ that runs this analysis on the iron ring directly [@paul2023stubborn]. The authors call the situation social captivity. The obligation on the page reads like a factual commitment, since the grammar (_I will not suffer bad workmanship_) is indicative. The indicative is what factual commitments use. In the practice of wearing the ring around, it is operating as an attitude expression of the kind Ayer and Stevenson were describing. Four months of sitting in that gap. From inside the gap I can claim a kind of technical objectivity while standing inside a moral structure that has formally declined, in its own constitutional papers, to be a moral structure at all.

There is a problem of dilution that keeps coming back to me. The behavioral case for the ring on the working hand is real enough. Thaler and Sunstein and the whole nudge literature have shown, over and over, that small physical reminders shift behavior at the margin, and a stainless steel ring catching against a sleeve is exactly the kind of reminder the literature is about. What undoes the case is that emotivism, as a behavioral system, depends on the people around you taking the expressed attitude seriously. Once everyone in the room reads the ring as a credential, the emotive content of it has bled out. The mechanism is right in the abstract. The room the mechanism is supposed to work in is not the room engineers are in.

The Frontiers paper concludes that the ceremony "has served to reproduce and map boundaries around engineering ethics and responsibility in Canada." Mapping the boundary was the function. Crossing it would have been the [[thoughts/ethics|ethics]].

<!-- institutional delusion -->

What bothers me about the ring is that I cannot find a falsification condition for it. There are half a million pinkies in the country wearing one of these things and not one of them, mine included, has been tested in a way that would tell its wearer whether the ritual did what the ritual claimed. I keep wanting to phrase that as an attack on the institution and what I actually mean is that I do not know how I would test mine either. The obligation says I will not suffer bad workmanship. An engineer signs off on a drawing he should not have signed off on. The next morning the ring is still there. A colleague notices nothing. The engineer notices nothing. The ring goes on structuring the engineer's self-perception and never gets put against anything in the world. Hare gave the name _blik_ to exactly this kind of unfalsifiable arrangement.[^hare] Flew added the parable about an invisible gardener who leaves no trace and is believed in anyway. The iron ring is a small piece of stainless wedded to a _blik_, with Flew's gardener thrown in for company.

[^hare]: From the Theology and Falsification symposium of 1950. Hare's position was that religious claims function as unfalsifiable attitudes that organize a believer's experience, and that everyone has a _blik_ of one kind or another. The lunatic who believes the dons want to kill him has one. The man who believes the dons do not want to kill him has one. The question is which _blik_ to wear. The iron ring is a _blik_ about engineering ethics, and Flew's challenge applies to it: the obligation dies the death of a thousand qualifications. The engineer who passed bad workmanship still wears the ring. The ring does not come off.

There have been half a million takers of this obligation in the hundred years it has been running. Things they signed off on have failed across that hundred years. Bridges and dams and pipelines and tailings ponds and the rest of it. Nobody has done the audit asking whether the ring made any of those incidents less likely. The profession does not want the audit. Doing the audit and finding nothing would require admitting that what we had wasn't engineering ethics but a piece of theatre, and the theatre has been useful enough to engineering that admitting this would also cost engineering something.

On April 25, 2025, the centennial of the first ceremony, the Corporation released a modernized version. They stripped the religious imagery and the gendered language. Two new poems were commissioned, one English and one French, to sit at the edges of the ceremony in place of the Kipling material. Chris Hadfield the astronaut was brought in to read a modern interpretation of the obligation in the style of a TED talk. The obligation text was preserved verbatim. The Kahnawake ironworkers were not named in the new ceremony any more than they had been in the old.

The modernization is the tell. If the wardens could swap the Kipling material out for a Hadfield voiceover without changing what the ceremony does, then the ethical content was never in the words around the obligation. The weight was carried by the ritual itself: the liminal hour, the placement of the ring on the working hand, the _communitas_ in the room. The ritual produces incorporation into a professional body. That is the dynamic running through [[posts/occupational licensure|the history of PEO]] across the longer view, and the modernization confirms it is what the ceremony has been doing the whole time.

Durkheim has the account of why a delusion like this gets traction at all [@durkheim1912elementary]. His word for the phenomenon is [[thoughts/performativity#political dimensions|collective effervescence]], meaning the emotional intensity that gathers in a room full of people performing a ritual at the same time. Inside that intensity, what's happening does feel like a moral transformation. I want to say something about this before going further. I was inside the intensity that evening, sitting in the room with the other engineers, and what I felt was not nothing. If I don't say so first the rest of this reads like a man patting himself on the back for having seen through a Victorian ceremony, which is the last position I want to be defending. The feeling was real enough. The mistake was treating the feeling as evidence of something other than itself. The institution counts on engineers running exactly that mistake at scale, and the emotivist position contains its own critique here, because if moral language is only feelings, then the ceremony delivered what its terms had advertised, and the error of having shown up expecting anything more substantial than feeling was on me.

The same logic runs through the degree. The clarity of deadlines was a scaffolding I could lean on while I built something else, and the scaffolding was a _blik_ in Hare's sense, an unfalsifiable self-image that organized my experience without ever requiring a test. For five years I lived inside the word _student_ as if it described what I was doing. At 11pm in an exam week, when I was triaging issues for Bento and not studying, _student_ was doing the same work that _engineer_ does for the man who has just had the ring put on his finger. Both demand nothing, which is what makes them comfortable.

Aristotle's three categories of friendship (utility, pleasure, virtue) read better as a test than as a typology, and the test resolves only when the two of you have come out of the same trajectory[^karlsson]. You find out which category your friendship was in when the other person ceases to be the thing the friendship was for. Almost none of my friendships from before the degree have survived the divergence. The two or three that have feel like the only ones I have ever had. I do not know yet whether two or three is going to be enough.

[^karlsson]: Borrowing the framing from Henrik Karlsson's ["Friends Missed"](https://www.henrikkarlsson.xyz/p/friends-missed). His point is that the test for which type a friendship was is whether it survives a context shift; you do not know in advance, you find out by losing.

Now the scaffolding is coming down and I do not know whether what I built can stand on its own.

Thinking about the wardens keeps sending me back to the Pullman novel and I have not been able to fully justify the mapping to myself. It is sloppy. It keeps surviving the times I try to think my way out of it. Haultain ends up in something like the Jesus position, in that he watched seventy-five men go into the St. Lawrence and spent the next decade or so trying to keep the next seventy-five from being his fault, and he died before the ceremony reached its hundredth taker. What lived on after him was the edited version the wardens kept producing, twenty-eight camps now, half a million pinkies under steel, a commemorative coin from the Royal Canadian Mint last April. The wardens are the ones with the wax tablet on this reading. They have been keeping Haultain's concern alive by hollowing it out the way Christ keeps Jesus alive by hollowing him out, and the stranger in the novel comes to Christ at night and tells him so, and Christ goes on writing anyway because the alternative is Jesus dying with the message and being forgotten.

Convocation is in June. The David Braley Athletic Centre at McMaster. I have gone into that gym every September since 2021 to renew course registration, and the degree will be handed to me in the same gym, which is funny in a way that does not translate well outside Hamilton. After convocation the deadlines stop being somebody else's problem. A couple of friends have told me that describing the shift in advance is supposed to help. It hasn't been. I have been describing it to myself since January and the describing has not done what the friends said it would do. The deadlines were a wall I leaned on for five years while I worked on something else. The wall is coming down on a calendar I did not set. Camus has the line about going on in full sight of the absurd, choosing again every morning, and what I keep thinking about lately is the morning part.

The ring stays on the pinky. It catches the desk when I type. In the shower the water bends a different way around steel than around the skin next to it, which I'm aware is not a piercing thing to notice after six thousand words on the subject. Haultain wanted tribal spirit. Tribal spirit is what the ring delivers. I've been the recipient of that delivery for four months now. Whether the engineer who has been wearing it has been doing his job is a separate question, and not the question the ring was built to be useful for.
