I shouldn't like to be the one who had to finger-snap the occasion into being in Toronto in the year 2026 (if only they'd let me retain the Victorian wording for the obligation I'm about to recite, by the way), but goodness, the room full of strangers and I — all of us a little breathless, a little smarter than average, all of us scrabbling through the performance to keep up with our own inadequacy, all of us pantomiming our common, parenthetical purpose — how does one not chuckle at the modern version? The bit with all the religious imagery stripped out? The Chris Hadfield-style modulation of ethos?" And by the way, there's that little obligation written down that we're supposed to read through word for word now... It's all right, I won't stop you."

And a sacramental kind of magic it is — the better for the long tradition of stating it as _ex opere operato_, as in old Catholic theology: the ring works its magic regardless of whether the wearer is particularly awful or merely indifferent or, sweet heaven, particularly good. A medieval loophole, a delicious edge of absurdity on which to stand your motor-vehicle engineering credentials... It's also a good favourite for a novelist, the thought that the ring's effects might be purely mechanical, might be built upon the ancient scaffolding of human dishonesty, might be adjudged anyway by fallen wardens whose epitaph the poet has chosen for them, "For even as Christ has power to edit Jesus as he chooses, so the wardens have power to edit Haultain as they choose..." So wrote Philip Pullman, Peter Wynn MacNeil, and their collaborators for _A Christmas Carol for Adult Video Rentals_, and waggishly so... For to what extent have either Christ or Haultain changed? How large a revision has been wrought at the great and terrible core?

The version of the Iron Ring ceremony we recur to now, as I say, would surprise poor Sir Joseph Adolphus Haultain, as it would surely surprise the bridge ironworkers, the Mohawks, who came over from Quebec to work on it and to whom it was partly dedicated before the bridge collapsed (ages ago now, you'll no doubt be thinking, so many assumptions were there on April 9th, 1907, that the new Quebec Bridge would be an awesome sextant of steel joined together, this great, swooping arch of gleaming light, 75 feet above the St. Lawrence, but that's me talking: I'm only telling you I remember the number 88 as I was growing up — 75 years ago — and I remember nothing else at all). It's almost endearing, this still lingering (on the official website) version of Haultain's tribal vision, this conviction, what with so many examinees dutifully coming and going, that the best way to maintain the one thread that ran through his career was not so much through a rigorous final exam as through a shared ritual, a matter of us-against-the-world superstition.

And isn't it delightful too that so many rings today are believed to have been made from the steel wreckage of the collapsed bridge? Hospital stainless, you see, not tragedy steel: a mere mix-up that has become a much-loved urban legend, passed from one budding engineer to another, inverting the truth while all the better appreciating it at the time.

Four months now I've lived with a ring on a pinky. I sort of forgot what it felt like when it was off, to stop feeling that little tug of absence, though who am I fooling, anyway? My one ring is just the latest addition to the daily rotation of hoop earrings and ear rings, anyway. There is a snap in my hoodie, now, every time I reach inside for my phone, there's a catch as I lift my glass of Cabernet, and there's that click as I deliver it — but the clicks have grown too regular for me to hear. The ring's just out there, like all the other shiny things. It's doing just fine, keeping company with my other jewelry, thank you, and I am only half-amused to think that maybe once or twice I've done that thing with my pinky that some of us do, like the way people doodle on their paper napkins if they don't have a pen, tapping the ring a little bit, but just a little bit, like you have one of those fancy old Rolexes on your wrist.

Meaning and obligation go together, don't they? Right there in the ceremony we're practically reading Ayer and Stevenson's emotivism, I have to say, that bit they had about moral theories being just a matter of expressing your attitude, or however the actual boot theory had it. Oh, not so very much, really. I suppose that is to be expected now the obligation's properly credentialled with a plaudit, and who is surprised that the ring is scarcely feeling its weight around your finger, is consequently rarely managed to nudge much of anywhere? And isn't it practically funny, this practical fail on the part of behavioral design, this nudge that does barely nudge, instead of nudging you into a proper fear of structurally inferior bridges as so many engineers feared on so many nights when the steel was lying cold in the harbours of Lake St. Louis?

But let's see, all right, let's get some good moral psychology going on down here, stick in the blik right from the beginning, the real delusion that haunts all rationalistic morality. Oh sure, what a nifty segue! Hare, Durkheim, whistle with me! The belief that there's something out there, that I should be this or should be that, a state of affairs necessarily true of the universe independently of how I myself feel. Sure, okay, but I know this is a dif, so let's scale back a bit, and just go with the don's paranoia. I don't know, I don't know. It's not like we don't also know that without the occasion of the meeting, and the persuasions conjured up by it, the eager imaginings we all had then, the ringing of the bells and the murmurs of our mothers through the walls, we couldn't very well still feel as we did, outside of it, at the height of it, that we were in on something, could we? It is the collective effervescence, the lo—thank you, what a nice feeling we're sharing at this ceremony, the one real high it will ever produce for any of us. Maybe it's just a coincidence, that all these hundred years or so of trains falling into the air, and hundred years of healers acting shady in the labs, and hundred years now and still there never seem to be any reprimands or revocations of any rings, but still.

But let's just give the 2025 modernization a pass. One by one, there'd be a few more showy edges gussied up and there'd be several fewer showy edges altogether, of course, and the Christ would be the wardens, and Haultain would just be some random editor needing to have a game be played as the Christ, but the rest would be the same. If for some reason it hadn't grown obvious from the Kipling line, we'd all have had it told to us up front that the point of the whole thing is the liminal hour, not the punctilious recitations and neither the sheaves of sentences about the joys of getting the overseers to keep the engineers in check at the construction sites — ha! Now, if the NMC had been a tad more crooked on their Kipling, and had left out the last six words, or the last ten, or so the original ones hadn't had a chance to live up to their claims — but who even thinks that far back anyway, right? The ethical core has never been in the words anyway. It was in the liminal hour, and it's in the feeling of tribe.

And as the ring goes on the hand, and still finding the word _student_ increasingly uncomfortable in its insistent nod to something that has been an agreeably familiar default blik for five years and is suddenly having to be abandoned bit by bit, and still thinking back on the Exam Week when a minute of attention paid to the laundry aside from the Bento triage was passed off as having come close to studying, and more broadly still, starting to miss having in the room people with whom the three kinds of friendship Aristotelian friends are meant to fit neatly, and being unsettled that the number that survives the loss of all shared obligations feels smaller than it perhaps ought.

One of these is still catching on the desk back in the gym where it had first felt the moisture of the shower there, still bending the water ever so slightly as it falls one mettlesome molecule at a time into the top of my washed hair, still keeping the physics of water from going exactly straight as it comes down, still insisting on its own cryptic presence on my left ring finger, still there, while Camus might come forth tomorrow morning on the GO train to Montreal, and the convocation under way at the athletics complex just blocks away.
