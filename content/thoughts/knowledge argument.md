Mary knows the color system from the outside: 650nm light, cone responses, V4, reports, dispositions. Then she leaves the room and sees a tomato. If something new arrives at that moment, physicalism has a bill to pay. [@jackson1982epiphenomenalqualia; @jackson1986whatmary]

## the move

1. Mary knows every physical fact about color vision.
2. Mary sees red for the first time and learns what red is like.
3. Knowledge is factive, so what she learns corresponds to a fact.
4. That fact was missing from the physical inventory.
5. [[thoughts/physicalism]] and bare [[thoughts/functionalism]] are incomplete.

the case works because the lab is clean. no bad neuroscience. no missing mechanism. the missing item is the felt character itself, the [[thoughts/qualia|quale]].

## what Mary might learn

**a new fact.** red has phenomenal character R. that gives the anti-physicalist exactly what they wanted, plus the bill: causal interaction, epiphenomenalism, and the problem of how private phenomenal facts enter public science. [@jackson1982epiphenomenalqualia]

**a new ability.** Lewis and Nemirow say Mary gains know-how: recognizing, remembering, imagining, comparing. [@lewis1988know; @nemirow1990physicalism] this keeps the ontology lean. it also feels evasive. the first sight of red seems to disclose what the ability is an ability with respect to.

**an old fact under a new grip.** Loar's phenomenal concept strategy says Mary knew the same fact through physical concepts and later grasps it through a phenomenal concept. [@loar1990phenomenal] this is the physicalist's cleanest exit. the cost is brute necessity: why this neural state feels like this remains unexplained.

## Jackson backs away

Jackson later dropped the argument. [@jackson1998argument] Epiphenomenalism made consciousness causally idle, evolutionarily odd, and epistemically strange. If experience causes no report and no belief, our knowledge of it becomes a tiny miracle.

so he takes the phenomenal-concept line: physical facts determine the phenomenal facts, but Mary could not possess the right first-person concept without the experience. she learns a new way of accessing an old physical fact.

that move saves physicalism by moving the gap into concepts. Chalmers's reply is that the gap still looks too deep for concept bookkeeping. [@chalmers1996consciousmind]

## pressure on [[thoughts/functionalism]]

the knowledge argument hurts functionalism because Mary knows the functional role before leaving the room. if the role exhausted the state, she should have the whole state already.

functionalists can expand the role until it includes phenomenal character. that wins by stipulation and loses the original reduction. or they can say the right abilities require the right system state, and Mary outside the room finally has it. that turns the fight into a question about whether instantiation is knowledge or experience. [@levin2024functionalism]

## why it matters for machines

the AI version is almost too clean. a text-only [[thoughts/LLMs|LLM]] can ingest color science, color poetry, design manuals, and reports from sighted people. it can answer the exam. the Mary question asks whether any of that is acquaintance with red.

a multimodal model moves closer to the room's exit. it gets pixels, labels, embeddings, and reward. that might be richer functional organization. it still does not settle whether there is phenomenal color on the inside.

[[thoughts/mechanistic interpretability]] sharpens the point. A circuit-level account can say which features track red, which heads route the information, and which downstream paths produce a report. [@geiger2025causalabstraction] That is access-level knowledge. Mary's argument says access-level knowledge can still miss the thing we meant by seeing red.

the note-to-self version: the argument is strongest when it stays small. Mary sees red. something changes. physicalism has to say exactly what changed without adding a second inventory of facts.
